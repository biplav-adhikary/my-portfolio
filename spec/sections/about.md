# About Section

## Purpose

A brief, genuine introduction. The reader should walk away knowing _how you think_, not just what you've built.

This section is the human bridge between the atmospheric hero and the concrete work below. It answers: "Who is this person, and why should I care?"

## Tone

Conversational. First person. Like a thoughtful answer to "So, tell me about yourself" — if the person asking genuinely wanted to know.

## Content Blocks

### 1. The Introduction

A short paragraph — 3–4 sentences max. Who you are and what you care about.

Not a bio. Not a timeline. A _statement of intent_.

> Draft: "I'm a software engineer who's spent the last five years building platforms where businesses connect, transact, and make sense of their data. My strongest instinct is on the frontend — architecture, interaction, the feel of an interface — but I've built APIs, data pipelines, and search infrastructure when the problem required it. I think a lot about the layer between system design and user experience. Most of my best work has been the kind that's invisible when it's done right."

### 2. How I Think

A few short points about your approach to work. These aren't skills — they're _values_.

- Architecture is a multiplier — the right structure makes every feature after it cheaper to build
- Own the problem end-to-end — if the best solution crosses the stack boundary, cross it
- Accessibility isn't a compliance task; it's a design constraint that makes products better for everyone
- Developer experience is a product too — build tooling, documentation, and shared libraries deserve the same care as user-facing features
- Question the requirement before building it — alignment with long-term product direction matters more than speed of delivery
- Ship the thing, then make it elegant — pragmatic delivery first, refinement as you go
- The best technical decisions are the ones you can explain simply to someone outside your team

### 3. Beyond Code (optional)

A brief, human note. Not hobbies-as-bullet-points. Something that adds dimension.

> Draft: "When I'm not coding, I'm usually noticing systems — how a city intersection manages traffic, how a game teaches mechanics without tutorials, how a conversation shifts when someone asks the right question. I think that instinct is what drew me to engineering in the first place."

## Layout Intent

- This section should feel open and spacious, not like a cramped bio card
- Consider an asymmetric layout — text on one side, a visual element (illustration, abstract nature motif) on the other
- The visual element should complement, not compete with, the text
- On mobile, stack gracefully — don't just shrink

## What to Avoid

- A photo with a corporate smile (if a photo is used, it should feel natural)
- "I am passionate about..." language
- Listing technologies here (that's for the Skills section)
- Making this section too long — it should be a doorway, not a lecture

## Open Questions

- Should this section include a small, hand-drawn style illustration?
- Is there a visual metaphor that works better than a sidebar image? (e.g., text winding around a nature element)
